The left takes over

The change in Mexico's political landscape

BY DIANA BAPTISTA

Published July 19, 2018

It took him 12 years to get there, but leftist candidate Andrés Manuel López Obrador got elected as the next President of México with a historical 53% of total votes. Widely viewed as an election where the population punished corrupt politicians of PRI, the party in power, López Obrador's victory changed the entire political landscape of the country.

Victory from north to south

López Obrador rose to power with Morena, a political party with only two years of existence that became the number one political power of the country. His victory was more significant in the state of Tabasco, where he was born. He was less popular in Guanajuato, a historically conservative state, where the vote for right-wing candidate, Ricardo Anaya, was bigger.

The leftist wave takes over Mexico

For the first time in 18 years of Mexican democracy, a left party gets control of the country.

José Antonio Meade, candidate of PRI, received a historically low percentage of votes, while independent candidate Jaime Rodríguez surprised with over 4% of total votes. Right-wing Ricardo Anaya finished in second place in spite of a controversial alliance between PAN and PRD, two idealogically opposite parties.

2006: A close fight

Right-wing PAN wins the election by less than 400,000 votes. Andrés Manuel López Obrador, supported back then by the PRD party, accuses fraud in the elections.

2012: PRI comes back

The PRI party returns to power after a 12 year hiatus. Previously, they ruled for 70 years through manipulated elections.

2018: Morena invades the country

In a record breaking election, Morena wins over 30 million votes. The right-wing wins in only one state. The PRI party doesn't win in any state.
